##post 1
AI is killing tactics faster than ever.
Good. Tactics expire. Marketers don’t.
Andrew Chen figured this out in 2012.
He called it the “Law of Shitty Clickthroughs”—every marketing tactic that works gets overused and then degrades over time.
The first banner ad ran a 78% clickthrough rate. Today you’d be thrilled with 0.1%.
AI doesn’t break that law. It speeds it up.
What used to cycle out in 18 months might now take 6 or less. 
AI comments are a perfect example. Early adopters may have gotten a visibility boost. Now using AI comments just make you look like a bot and torches your reputation. The effectiveness of that tactic closed in months, not years.
But you know what? This is actually great news if you’re a creative marketer.
Because while AI can execute current trends at scale, it can’t predict what will work next before everyone else feels it.
As the cycle speeds up, creativity becomes even more valuable.
So rejoice, marketers. We’ll live to fight another day.
P.S. “The Law of Shitty Clickthroughs” is a coined problem—one of 6 types of ownable ideas.
And the fact that I’m still citing it 14 years later just shows the power of this stuff. Andrew’s writing is what got him noticed by Marc Andreessen and Ben Horowitz, landed him a VP role at Uber, and made him a general partner at a16z managing a $660M fund.
That’s what happens when you stop parroting your peers and start owning ideas.

##post 2
Brand gets you on the shortlist. 
Buyability gets you the client.
There's a lot of debate in the marketing world about the importance of brand versus product marketing.
One of those silly nonsense debates I can't help but wade in on.
In terms of what I do with buyability and offer packaging, my work is more on the product side of the equation.
But I also see brand as a fourth bonus D to my buyability model.
The three core D's (desired, different, decisive) all speak directly to your offer. Which for consultants essentially is your product.
But the bonus D - discoverable - is about what comes before the offer.
It's how people know you exist, and why they add you to their shortlist in the first place.
And that's where brand comes in.
For the (mostly) solo consultants I work with, this expresses itself more as a personal brand. Which let's face it is the same thing just applied to a person.
I like to see brand as shorthand for "perception". How does the market perceive you?
The aim is to be perceived as the go-to expert in your space. And you can influence that perception through:
— Your visual brand identity (like my pink colour)
— Your ownable idea (shoutout to Katelyn Bourgoin for helping me with mine)
— Your content
Amongst other things. 
Buyability is then how you can make sure that awareness turns into actual clients. By framing your offer around a problem (desired), showing why they should choose you (different), and giving all the info they need (decisive).

In other words, brand helps you fill your pipeline.
Buyability helps you turn that into clients.
